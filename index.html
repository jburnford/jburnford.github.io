About

I am an environmental and digital historian of Britain, Canada, and the British World during the long nineteenth century. My research explores the connections between rapid industrialization and urbanization in London and environmental transformations across the globe, using advanced computational methods including Historical GIS, Linked Open Data, and large language models to analyze large-scale historical corpora.

I am an Associate Professor in the Department of History at the University of Saskatchewan, where I teach environmental, transnational, and digital history. I am a co-editor of Historical Methods and ActiveHistory.ca, and a faculty member at the Historical GIS Lab.

My book West Ham and the River Lea: A Social and Environmental History of London's Industrialized Marshland, 1839-1914 (UBC Press, 2017) examined how environmental degradation in London's industrial marshlands sparked political change. Ongoing projects include a collaboration with Stéphane Castonguay and Cheryl Troupe tracing Britain's "ghost acres" in the forests and ports of the St. Lawrence Valley and Saskatchewan. This current research involves the development of knowledge graphs and text-mining pipelines to explore commodity networks in nineteenth-century at scale. I also co-led Remember Rebuild Saskatchewan, an interdisciplinary project documenting the pandemic's impact on health equity through community archives and oral histories.

Work in Progress

Philosophical Transactions Archive (1665-1869)

OCR digitization of the world's first scientific journal. 8,128 articles across 106,829 pages from the Royal Society of London, featuring work by Newton, Hooke, Faraday, Darwin, and 3,920 other authors. Available in plain text and JSON formats.

Early Encyclopaedia Britannica Corpus (1771-1842)

OCR digitization of the first seven editions of the Encyclopaedia Britannica. Over 174,000 articles spanning 71 years of documented knowledge, from the 3-volume 1st edition to the 21-volume 7th edition.

Sir Joseph Banks Knowledge Graph (1765-1820)

Interactive visualizations of Sir Joseph Banks' correspondence networks. 7,124 documents involving 6,441 people, with network graphs, correspondence flows, and topic/commodity analysis.
